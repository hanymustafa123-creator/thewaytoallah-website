الغنة — Ghunna Rule (Shaddah on Nūn / Mīm)

When a نّ (Nūn with shaddah) or مّ (Mīm with shaddah) appears in the text, you must hold a nasal resonance (ghunna) for 2 counts (harakatayn). This is also called "the nasal tone of the reinforced letter." The sound comes from the nose — imagine humming through your nose while holding the letter.

B الوقف على أواخر الكلمات — How to Stop (Waqf) at End of Words (Rules for pausing/stopping in recitation)

الوقف — Waqf: Stopping at the End of Words

When you stop (waqf) on a word at the end of a breath, specific rules apply:
• A final تنوين (tanwīn) — nunation — disappears and is replaced by a sukūn. E.g. كِتَابٌ → كِتَابْ
• A final تاء مربوطة (tā' marbūṭah ة) is pronounced as a hā' (هـ). E.g. رَحْمَةٌ → رَحْمَهْ
• A final alif maqsūra (ى) is held without tanwīn.
• A final مدّ (madd) before a stop is lengthened to 2 or 4 or 6 counts depending on the type.
• You never stop in the middle of a meaningful unit — stop at natural breath/sense pauses.

✦ Tafseer Notes — The Word "قَرِينٌ" in Sūrat Qāf (Scholarly opinions on the meaning)

Word in focus: قَرِينٌ / قَرِينَهُ — "companion / associate"

The word قَرِين (plural: قُرَنَاء) means a "companion," "associate," or "inseparable partner." It appears in Sūrat Qāf at verse 23 and verse 27, with scholars holding different views on who or what this "companion" refers to in each context.

📖 Verse 23 — وَقَالَ قَرِينُهُ هَٰذَا مَا لَدَيَّ عَتِيدٌ

"And his companion will say: This is what I have ready [to present]."

📚 Opinion 1 — Ibn 'Abbās, Qatādah, Mujāhid
The "companion" (قرين) here refers to the angel assigned to record the person's deeds (كاتب الأعمال — the recording angel). This angel presents the full record of the person's actions on the Day of Judgment, saying: "Here is what I have ready — the complete account." This is supported by the context: the verse follows mention of a "driver" and a "witness" (سائق وشهيد) accompanying each person.
📚 Opinion 2 — Ibn Mas'ūd, al-Ḍaḥḥāk
The "companion" refers to the devil (shayṭān / qareen) assigned to the person throughout their life, who now testifies against them. The devil says: "This is what I have ready" — meaning, the sins and disobedience I led this person into are all documented and presented. This links to the Quranic concept of the shayṭan being each person's "qareen" (قرين) in Q 43:36.
📚 Summary
Most classical scholars (including al-Ṭabarī and Ibn Kathīr) consider Opinion 1 (the angel) stronger because of the grammatical context and the flow from verse 21 onward, which speaks of angels assigned to drive and witness. However, Opinion 2 remains valid and is not contradicted by the text.

📖 Verse 27 — قَالَ قَرِينُهُ رَبَّنَا مَا أَطْغَيْتُهُ وَلَٰكِن كَانَ فِي ضَلَالٍ بَعِيدٍ

"His companion [devil] said: 'Our Lord, I did not make him transgress, but he himself was in far-gone error.'"

📚 Opinion 1 — Majority of Commentators (Ibn Kathīr, al-Ṭabarī, al-Qurṭubī)
In verse 27, the context clearly shifts: the "companion" here is the shayṭān (devil / personal demon) assigned to the disbeliever. The devil disowns responsibility on the Day of Judgment, saying "I did not force him into sin" — this matches the Quran's consistent portrayal of Iblīs disclaiming responsibility (cf. Q 14:22). The devil merely whispered and invited; the human freely chose to go astray.
📚 Opinion 2 — Some Scholars (cited in al-Rāzī)
A minority view holds that in verse 27 the "companion" could still refer to the angel of deeds, who says: "I did not oppress him in what I recorded — I only wrote what he did." Under this view, the angel is defending the accuracy of the record. However, most scholars find this reading less convincing grammatically and contextually, since the statement "I did not make him transgress" fits the shayṭān's defense far better.
📚 Key Takeaway
Verse 23: The qareen is most likely the recording angel, presenting the full record.
Verse 27: The qareen is almost certainly the shayṭān, who rejects responsibility for the human's sin.
Both verses together paint a complete picture of the Day of Reckoning: the record is presented, and then the assigned devil distances himself — leaving the sinner with no excuse.
